# Acts of Kindness

*By [[Brian Conn (Author)]]*

There's a local legend from Connacht, during World War II, when the Republic of Ireland was in its infancy, and not wanting to give more of their young men to die for England or any other piece of land except their own, the fledgling nation's leaders had declared Ireland to be neutral in that conflict.

Connacht is the province that makes up the northwest of the island, and the land is carved up with several fjords, which technically are deeper than they are wide, and provided a clever hiding place for submarine captains in the contested waters surrounding the British Isles. One night, a British and a German submarine ended up entering the same fjord, and surfaced to freshen their air. At daybreak, each discovered the presence of the enemy, and nearly simultaneously, the discovery was made by villagers in their homes on the edge of the lake. Whether it was because they had discovered each other in neutral territory, or because something about their relative position in the fjord put them each at a disadvantage, neither sub crew attacked the other.

In the same instant that this unspoken ceasefire was occurring, the villagers put two boats into the water, stocked with fresh milk and eggs and other things submariners were accustomed to living without, and rowed out to the warships as a gesture of good will. The subs gratefully took on the supplies, and the crews of each spent the day at rest, enjoying the gifts that had been brought on board. This accidental truce, enhanced and perhaps extended by the Irish villagers, lasted all day, and after nightfall the boats eased back under the surface and slipped out of the fjord to return to their conflict.

Some who hear this story, or even the simple fact of Ireland's neutrality during that war, are angered by the Irish. Were the Nazis not clearly committing atrocities and swallowing up the whole of Europe in their campaign of aggression? Were the Allies not fighting a righteous war, attempting to save not just themselves but the rest of the free world from the Third Reich? Some reports suggest because of Ireland's oppression under British rule, that German subs were using Ireland to secretly refuel in their seek-and-destroy campaign in the seas surrounding England.

Regardless of the political motivations behind the official declaration of neutrality, I believe the practical exercise of that neutrality in the fjord that day was not a statement of a political nature. It was a statement that rose above politics and patriotism and legalities.

When the villagers looked out and saw two warships floating in their fjord, they didn't see men bent on destroying their enemy just yards away. They didn't see a power who, if it prevailed, might very easily return one day to occupy this island; nor did they see a power who, until very recently, had for eight centuries been occupying it, brutally suppressing any attempts at self-governance. No, they saw sailors, probably not too different from their own sons who spent a dangerous life out there on the seas, and they showed those sailors hospitality, carrying them the things they knew their boys would want if they came in from a long stint aboard a small ship.

Should they have intervened? Should they have helped one and not the other? Could they in doing so have changed the fate of any of those on board the two ships or anyone else? Probably not. It's impossible to know.

What seems clear to me is that the villagers had a choice: to aid in "the war effort" of either combatant, to duck and cover and hope they left quickly, or to simply recognize the dignity of the humans who came into their little corner of the world and do their best to treat them as they would want to be treated.

To me it is as Christlike as any modern story I've ever heard.

The hospitality of the Irish is legendary, and I've been privileged to receive it on a few memorable occasions. Stories like these reinforce this legend, that there is something deep in the core of the Irish culture, as fundamental to the nation's people as their struggle for independence, and the exuberance with which they celebrate what life they have. But the particular gift they have for hospitality is one with its roots in the strange lessons taught by a man two thousand years before and as many miles away. Don't just love your friends, he said. Everybody does that. No, I'm telling you to love your neighbor as yourself AND even love your enemies. Provide for the least among you, the widows, the foreigners, people you don't owe anything to or know anything about. If someone strikes your cheek, show him the other to strike as well. If they steal your coat, give them your shirt, too.

These lessons are so odd that if a group of people were to follow them, and live them out, they would truly be a conspicuous group. Would they seem odd to the others around them who didn't follow the same guidance? Indeed they would. Would they be subject to persecution? Almost certainly. But if enough of them were to gather that they could create a society that honored this guidance, surely they would have the freedom to exist without undue persecution.

But would having that comfort absolve them of the need to follow the original guidance?

There is a war in our culture. It has combatants on both sides. It has been called a war "for the soul of our nation." It isn't a war with bombs and bullets, but there is still violence being done to the minds, hearts, and souls of the combatants. And some of the combatants are not volunteers; they are drafted, conscripted, or simply forced to fight in the war, because they find themselves under attack. I work at a university that enjoys the freedom to organize its entire mission around the claims and life of Jesus Christ. Having declared this as our intent, we find ourselves under attack in this culture war. Do we assume, as many do, that because we are under attack, then we must fight? In spite of the foundational guidance of the One we serve?

The thing about a war is that it often harms noncombatants who get caught in the crossfire. As a university, our job is to educate young people. As part of our enhanced mission, these young people are not just pupils, they are trusting us to create an environment in which they can grow in their faith during the process of gaining a degree, at which time they will go out into a complex world and give it their all. These students are among the people most at risk of becoming collateral damage.

My neighbor believes the earth is flat. We had a long talk about it. He wasn't interested in knowing why I believe Earth is spherical. But I was very interested in hearing the basis for his beliefs and was surprised to find that when I asked probing questions, he kept going back to scripture for answers to many of my questions. "It's all there in the Bible," he said. If he was just a loner, thinking odd thoughts, thinking his neighbor the round-Earth guy is an idiot, I'd probably be fine with that. But he's not a loner. He's got two energetic kids barely old enough for grade school. So I don't worry about him. I worry about when they get older and decide that the flat earth thing doesn't hold water, do they lump all of dad's crazy ideas about faith and science and scripture together and decide it all must be the ravings of a madman. Maybe not. Maybe if some of the basic wisdom in scripture continues to ring true to them, they will be able to distinguish the Truth from the error in some of dad's thinking. But if they never get a glimpse of that basic truth, the irresistible power of love and mercy, the whole document will be so much impotent scribbling.

It's the same fear I have for our students in this culture war. Who says we have to fight the war? What do we say about the neutrality inherent in the commands of the Lord Jesus Christ? If our students see us dealing damage in the name of Jesus, how does that pan out in the long run? They see the contradiction. It's quite obvious. Dealing blows to an enemy to protect your right to love your enemy?

What I'm proposing is not giving up our right to the free exercise of our religion. I am proposing that we don't cease the exercise thereof. I'm proposing that we row out just as swiftly and offer unmitigated hospitality and regard to the sailor we find in our fjord, regardless of what flag he or she is flying.

I'm not proposing that we change the way we read the Bible. I'm not proposing that we throw out our beliefs. I'm proposing that we pray for the strength and wisdom to parse through the numerous passages that make up the whole Truth, and together with the inspiration and guidance from the Living God in our lives, take a stand for kindness. It's become a terribly complex world. We should approach it boldly and simply with the conspicuous oddity of unconditional love.
