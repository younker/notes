## My Stance

__Sent to Kat and Kristyn on Dec. 10, 2025__

It is my understanding that specialty pharmacies are contractually and procedurally required to verify coverage, confirm payment arrangements, and obtain a patient’s informed consent for any financial responsibility prior to dispensing and shipping medication.¹ PrudentRx has stated that it did not authorize payment for these prescriptions, indicating that the pharmacy dispensed the medication without completing the required verification and approval process.

I acted at all times in good faith and in reasonable reliance on the pharmacy’s established dispensing practices and the insurer’s benefit administration systems, based on my longstanding experience with this medication and process. I had no reason to believe there were any issues related to coverage, eligibility, or payment, particularly given that the medication was shipped to me on multiple occasions. Such shipments customarily occur only after coverage, eligibility, and any required patient consent have been confirmed.

Accordingly, this matter reflects an administrative and operational failure on the part of the pharmacy, not any attempt on my part to obtain medication without coverage or authorization. Any failure by the pharmacy to perform its contractual and procedural obligations should not result in the imposition of unanticipated financial liability upon me.

¹ Specialty pharmacies’ obligations to verify eligibility, coverage, prior authorization, and patient financial responsibility before dispensing medication arise from their contractual agreements with PBMs and insurers, applicable state pharmacy regulations, federal billing and consumer-protection requirements, and established industry standards of care. Under these frameworks, dispensing medication without completing the required verification or obtaining informed financial consent constitutes an internal administrative failure for which the pharmacy, not the patient, bears financial responsibility.
