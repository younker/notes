# Too much or not enough?

**2 messages** from 2019-10-03 to 2019-10-03

## 1. 2019-10-03 00:45 — heathergoffinet@hotmail.com

Tricia,
Thank you for the reply. However, we are disappointed with the response. Frankly, it's not enough and leaves us feeling even more strongly that the school is not taking this situation seriously.

You suggest that we should continue to problem solve and monitor behavior. The only monitoring I have witnessed is when you were popping in classes and lunch throughout the day the week of Sept 17. Yes, we appreciate that you took the time to do this, but it was a snapshot in time, not a longer-term effort. The monitoring by the school at present consists of Susan's recess duty. Therefore, the only person the school is having monitor the situation to ensure our children's safety on a daily basis has plainly stated that she does not believe there is an issue, has minimized our concerns for the safety of our son, even after given the witness of a real possibility of immediate danger during recess, and has even ridiculed our taking Rohan's threats seriously. This is not a solution.

Yes, we understand that recess can be a difficult time to monitor interactions. That is why I am at every recess, circle time, lunch and any other time Rohan is with Stephen. This incident at recess occurred in front of the two adults monitoring recess. Despite this, I still had to press Susan to intervene and handle the concern. Interactions at recess are not difficult to monitor if the adult in charge is taking their responsibility of monitoring a threatening situation seriously. Without any additional information or details, if the person designated by the school to monitor the boys only has even the most basic info - Rohan threatening to bring a knife to school and kill Stephen and then Rohan being seen sharpening a sharp object on a stone and approaching Stephen from behind, then making obvious efforts to conceal the object more than once from the adult on duty - there is an obvious intervention that must happen to ensure the safety of not only Stephen but other children at recess. It is alarming that all of this played out WITH me right there. Imagine the possible outcomes had I not been there?

Seven days ago there was an 8-year-old from Texas been unconscious by a couple of his fellow third graders in the bathroom. One day prior to that there was a boy from California who died after sustaining injuries from being beat by two of his middle school peers. These are only two stories of many that are happening in our schools every week. The rhetoric used by school systems is all the same. For our own, it is "Bainbridge Island School District is committed to a safe and civil educational environment for all students, employees, parents/legal guardians, volunteers, and patrons, that is free from harassment., intimidation or bullying." We have a situation here that has been ongoing for over a year and has included*, physical harm on multiple occasions, perpetual name calling, multiple death threats, intimidation to only name a few, and is being done by a student who has a history of failed school attempts dating all the way back to preschool, displays of solitary social behavior, fits of rage, displays of non-verbal anger in the classroom, regular submersion in violent video games, bullying younger kids, lack of accountability of behavior, blaming behavior, transference of own behavior, and even witnessed encouragement by his dad to assert aggression towards Stephen, who as a reminder is only 7 years old! Even if you think the possibility that Rohan would follow through on his threat is remote, there are an alarming number of indicators and red flags that demand this situation should be taken seriously. So we have to ask - where is the school's action behind the verbal commitment outlined above?

*examples provided to school on previous occasions occurring Sept 2018 - present. There are many other examples not indicated here.
Physical harm - 9/12 Rohan choked Stephen holding him down by his neck for multiple seconds, Sept 2018 we discovered at the end of the month that Stephen had been wearing 3-4 sets of clothes to school because Rohan along with other boys encouraged by Rohan to throw the sharp gumballs from the tree at him.
Name calling - From Sept 2018 to April 2019 Rohan called Stephen 'idiot', 'twerp' and 'moron' on a weekly basis.
Death threats - May 28th Rohan said he was going to kill Stephen. Sept 12th Rohan said after my daughter, Emily, interrupted him choking Stephen, "I am going to bring a knife to school and finish you off". This same day Stephen asked Rohan why he had a problem with him to which Rohan replied, "My problem is that you exist"
Intimidation - May 2019 Rohan chased Stephen outside of the playground fences and refusing to let him back in (Ian was present) Sept 2019 Rohan seen on multiple occasions circling Stephen's area of play

Yes, we will fill out the HIB form for the incidents and submit it. We are wondering why this procedure was not followed when the incident was reported on 9/13? Or for that matter why was the HIB form not offered on June 1st to document the incident from May 28th when Rohan threatened to kill Stephen? Or last fall when Jason spoke to Martha about Rohan bullying Stephen. In our meeting on Sept. 17th, I asked how we could document the incidents and efforts to which you replied, "yes, we should probably do that." I found the HIB form myself only after receiving the Commodore Newsletter on Oct 1st - I happen to click on the BISD Family Handbook and stumbled upon the form. We are then left wondering why procedure was not followed to ensure adequate documentation?

With the exception of our initial meeting on Sept 17th, we have felt that our son's safety and the prevention of Rohan causing future harm is not being taken seriously nor acted on by the school. I have on multiple occasions felt that we as the victims are being victimized all over again by the school. Susan's ridicule and denial of an issue even being present only confirmed what we were already feeling. We understand that this situation presents an element of pitting the word of one student against the word of the other. We have tried to inform the school and document the incidents the best we can. It is alarming that despite a highly publicized bullying epidemic running rampant across our schools and in the age of the #MeToo movement, the voices of our victims are still struggling to be heard and to be taken seriously. I have been especially disappointed in the responses by a school that champions educational options for families, and by a program, like Mosaic, that touts kindness, empathy and compassion among it's key tenants.

Again, we would like to request a meeting to discuss options to address this issue and ensure the safety of our children.
We look forward to your thoughtful response and proposed next step.
-Heather and Jason Younker
