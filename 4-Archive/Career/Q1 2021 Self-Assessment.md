- **Target:** Apr 2021

## Purpose of this document

As part of Moz's promotion process, the candidate for promotion must take some time to reflect on what attributes of the target level they already practice and what aspects they are continuing to focus on. This self-assessment will help your manager evaluate if you are ready to be promoted to the next level. Although this feedback will also be viewed by other managers at Moz, it is ultimately your manager's decision whether or not the promotion will happen.

When writing up your assessment, refer to: Moz Engineering Leveling Guide

We don't expect a specific format for your responses. Your goal is to communicate your self-assessment clearly, thoroughly, and with supporting details.

## Assessment

### General Assessment

> Read through the summary paragraph describing the proposed new level. Do you believe your work adheres to the spirit of that summary? How? Share stories that highlight your contributions at this higher level of expectation.

I would not allow the new level if I did not believe I met the criteria. Please see below for ample stories highlighting my contributions.

### Technical Ability

> Technical ability is a critical part of any individual's contribution on a team. Describe your technical contributions and how they align with the expectations of the new level.

My greatest contribution over the latest review period is that of the formation ETL pipeline. This effort has demonstrated technical proficiencies in the following areas:

- Data transfer: eg. APIs/HTTP, container-to-container over TCP, distributed systems, etc
- Rapid development & proof-of-concepts: eg SoV, METL API, lambda (language)
- Architecture: how METL is constructed but also the impact to (up|down)stream systems

The language and METL work, each a complex array of initiatives and considerations, demonstrate the depth of my understanding of our systems as well as how I am driving and contributing the conversations to advance Moz into the next phase of it's technological development.

### Communication

> Communication is critical in extending the value of an individual's contribution beyond the editor. Share how your communication has added value to your work, the team, or the organization at large.

I have gone to great lengths to increase communication across not just engineering but also, specifically, with product, regarding an array of concerns including language, storage, formats, architecture, application boundaries, system requirements and limitations, boundary pushing solutions, project pages, excessively documenting PoC findings and other misc. engineering problems which may go unnoticed by other teams.

An example would be the Moz ETL pipeline project as seen over the last 9 months. The ideas surrounding this novel architecture and, much more broadly speaking, pattern for accessing data, has been communicated:

- through multiple ARs
- several non-technical presentations from the IC level up to Scott
- several technical presentations to teams, managers, ICs, senior engineers and various combinations
- working with our principal as well as other level 5s to increase my effectiveness and identify flaws in my communication style

Our involvement in the AR process has also been substantial. From pushing and defining what an AR is and is not (ie language introduction vs AR0/1) to working with Anish and Overcash to hopefully develop a new style of AR (eg the style used in the METL AR2), this team is helping the company to shape ideas about how we can work together.

### Planning & Execution

> Share how you've demonstrated your ability to plan work and then execute against that plan. Think of times where you have effectively clarified requirements, worked productively to meet impactful deadlines, or demonstrated the ability to own challenging work.

The most obvious example here is METL. This initiative has required so many different forms of planning and execution that it is hard to recount and communicate them all. For example, the first portion of this was the SoV proof-of-concept work which would segway into the language debate. This small step required I work with...

- product to identify a proper candidate
- my manager to identify the proper resources
- engineers to identify the proper implementation candidates
- the MozNext group to communicate findings and gather feedback
- our principal engineer to gain feedback, justify decisions and uncover unknown unknowns
- confluence in order to document *all* of the things
- engineering managers, principals and senior engineers to communicate findings and solicit feedback
- the boarder engineering organization to communicate the findings

The next step was the language debate which offered no defined process or format and followed an ill-defined and hasty cohort language decision. While the communication requirements were the same, they differed in priority and consideration. For example, there was less communication with the MozNext committee and much, much more communication with engineers, especially those outside our cohort. Thus we found ourselves in a bifurcated conversation:

1. We were pushing to define the process of introducing a new language to the engineering org (while introducing a new language to the engineering org)
2. Challenge the existing decisions that were made based on a new process we believed to be heavily flawed

The planning, execution and communication for this process well exceeded anything we expected or believe to be appropriate or tolerable. And with the excessive planning and effort (execution), we did help to redefine the process and arrive at a good decision for our team/cohort (and, I believe, Moz).

At this point, we launched into the pipeline proof-of-concepts. This required much of the communications outlined above, in varied degrees, but was complicated by additional factors including:

- This was far less official than the SoV or language process
- Introduced a dramatically broader x-team impact
- There were guardrails preventing certain (official) forms of communication, presentation and feedback. This required informal discussions with engineers to gather criteria and details about their systems that we did not understand
- Upstream services (ie SERP collection) required consideration due to long-term impact and usage on their system
- It was complicated by continually being deprioritized
- Required more non-engineering communication including beloved keynote presentations and director-level conversations

At this point, I spend much of my time in the planning and execution phase and dramatically less in the engineering.

### Maturity

> In what ways have you taken efforts to understand your work environment and affect positive change, i.e. what kinds of suggestions around work agreements, development practices, etc. have you worked towards? How have you shown a capacity for disciplined growth?

Being the tech lead for the data services team over the last couple of years has helped me to mature in a number of areas. This includes:

- **Creating a safe place:** When you create a safe space, all kinds of good things follow and people tend to flourish into their best possible self.
- **Continuous learning:** this is something every engineer should do. However, I find that as I get older I battle the notion of becoming a relic, a dinosaur who always does things the same old way. Our field is constantly changing and each of us must change with it but as a tech lead and somebody with influence over the engineering organization, I believe there is an even stronger need to stay on top of things in order to keep pushing us forward to do things better
- **Mentoring:** I hate to say it but I am not a 10x developer nor do we have any 10x devs on our team, group or organization. However, if we take developers where they are and increase the tools/skills, attitude and drive they have, we can turn them into 2 or 3x developers. Do that a few times and you have something much better, stronger and resilient than a single 10x developer. This approach to team building is something I am just starting to really understand and hopefully, learn how to realize.
- **Advocate (and challenging) points of view**
- **Emotions, passion and communication:** Admittedly, I am an emotional being and my passion can often be misinterpreted to the detriment of the cause I am pursuing. However, with guidance from Anish and Victoria, I am learning where, how and when to demonstrate that in order to be more effective
- **Communication:** As I strive to be a stronger advocate for our team and influencer in the company, it has been impressed upon me the importance of hand shaking and baby kissing. The need for communication to meet this particular end has not been my responsibility in the past, however, I am building up those skills to increase my effectiveness.

### Autonomy

> Do you believe that you make correct judgements and accomplish what you set out to do? Why?

Do I make correct judgements? If I didn't know better I would think that a trick question. Software engineering is a never-ending series of tradeoffs. This fact presents a double-edged sword where on the one hand, it broadens the field of "correct" actions while on the other, it leaves you open to criticism and judgement regardless of the decision you make.

Enough philosophy. Do I believe I make correct judgements? Yes. 100% of the time. I strive to set personal feelings, thoughts, beliefs aside when making decisions that impact the team, engineering and Moz. In hindsight, there are some decisions which were made with missing or incomplete data but the vast majority seem to be the "right" choice (where "right" equates to decisions I would make again).

When judging past decisions I typically ask questions similar to the following:

- Did we do our due diligence? This would include:
  - seeking out information from all sources
  - understanding the problem before considering a solution
  - Planning execution before putting pen to paper
- Have my decisions helped us to meet our core initiatives?
- Can we build off the existing implementation. In some cases this may, counterintuitively, mean using the first iteration to learn more about the problem knowing that we will discard most/all of the actual implementation in order to improve (eg DDMR volume implementation)
- Have my decisions helped to strengthen our team and the engineering org and Moz in general?

The second part of that question asks if I "accomplish what I set out to do?". I have to say no, not always. There are always obstacles to accomplishing even simple tasks. These factors often include company (change of) direction, peer/other biases, inability to properly carve out time and/or resources, etc. The one thing I can say is that I excel at pushing an agenda I believe in. Even to a fault I will continue to push if I believe it is the correct thing to do. An example would be MDAPI. That project was predicated on the fact Moz should be a consumer of it's own data; the benefits are numerous. However, I did not accomplish that task due to influences outside my control. However, 2½ years later I am still pushing to meet that goal because I believe it is the correct approach both from a business/product perspective but also from an architectural perspective.

I do not always accomplish my task but I am relentless pursuing it.
