## Pseudo Gout -- Maybe

...form of arthritis characterized by sudden, painful swelling in one or more of your joints. These episodes can last for days or weeks.
Crystal deposits within a joint cause both (pseudo and actual gout) conditions, although the type of crystal differs for each condition.
...(symptoms) mimic those of rheumatoid arthritis
Attacks -- swollen, warm & severely painful

*Why Yes?*
- "The most commonly affected joint is the knee" -- this thing commonly happens to one or both knees
- "commonly affects the knee or wrist" -- wrist pain is my most common pain
- "can involve the hips, shoulders, elbows, finger joints, toes, or ankles" -- within the last month, has happened to shoulders
- "Trauma to a joint... increases your risk of pseudogout in that joint" -- applies to R shoulder, R knee and ankles but not L shoulder, thumb, elbow
- "people who have excessive calcium or iron in their blood or too little magnesium" -- I am taking 1k mg vitatmin C twice a day
- kidney failure can contribute -- I have (had?) IgA nephropathy

*Followup*
- underactive thyroid gland?
- overactive parathyroid gland?
- To diagnose, fluid is removed from the inflamed joint and analyzed under a microscope. The presence of CPP crystals indicates pseudogout

## Bursitis -- Maybe
- painful condition that affects the small, fluid-filled sacs 
- common locations for bursitis are in the shoulder, elbow and hip. But you can also have bursitis by your knee, heel and the base of your big toe
- often occurs near joints that perform frequent repetitive motion
- Treatment typically involves resting the affected joint (worked for L wrist)

*Causes*
- repetitive motions
- positions that put pressure on the bursae around a joint
- injury or trauma to the affected area
- inflammatory arthritis such as rheumatoid arthritis

*Diagnosis*
- often diagnose bursitis based on a medical history and physical exam
- possible imaging tests (ultrasound or MRI)
- blood tests or an analysis of fluid from the inflamed bursa

*Treatment*
- Bursitis generally gets better on its own
- rest, ice and taking a pain reliever can relieve discomfort
- corticosteroid

*Consult your doctor if you have*
- Disabling joint pain
- Sudden inability to move a joint
- Sharp or shooting pain, especially when you exercise or exert yourself
