## Coaching Philosophy

**Approach in one or two sentences:** Develop the player, not the result. My job is to create the conditions where kids build real competence, fall in love with the game, and want to come back next season.

**Who do you aspire to be as a coach?** Someone players remember not because we won a particular game, but because they stuck with the sport, grew as people, and felt seen as individuals.

**One value or idea that's important to you:** Long-term participation over short-term outcomes. Brad Stulberg's piece on Norway captures it — they delay scorekeeping until 13, encourage multi-sport play, and keep youth sports accessible. The result is both 93% youth participation and consistent Olympic dominance. The "develop kids" vs. "win now" tradeoff isn't really a tradeoff.

## Motivation for Coaching

**Why did you decide to become a coach?** I had coaches who shaped me, and a few who almost drove me out of the game I loved. I want to be the former for somebody else's kid.

**Main reason kids play soccer:** Because it's fun. If it stops being fun, they stop showing up. The US sits around 50% youth sports participation; Norway is at 93%. The difference is what the adults choose to optimize for.

**What should a coach do for their players?** Help them get better, treat them like individuals, and protect the part of them that loves the game.

## Player-Centered Coaching

**What does 'player-centered coaching' mean?** Starting from what this specific kid needs — technically, physically, emotionally — instead of running a one-size-fits-all program and sorting the players who happen to fit it.

**How do you help players get better?** Age-appropriate session design, individual feedback, and a lot of touches on the ball. Practices should look like the game so kids are solving real problems, not just running drills.

**One way you get to know your players:** I talk to them about things that aren't soccer — other sports they play, weekends, school. The relationship comes before the coaching.

**How do you let players share ideas or make choices?** I ask questions instead of giving answers when I can ("what did you see?" "what could we try differently?"), and I hand them ownership over things like warm-ups, small-sided formats, and end-of-practice scrimmage rules.

## Goals for the Course

**One or two things you want to learn or improve:** (1) Sharpening session design — more high-quality reps in less time. (2) Better tools for measuring player development that aren't just wins and losses.

## Expectations for the Course

**What do you hope educators and fellow coaches do?** Push back on my assumptions and share what's actually worked for them — including the failures, which usually teach more than the wins.

**One way you can help make the course a good place to learn:** Show up prepared, ask honest questions, and share what I've learned the hard way — including what I'd do differently if I were starting over.

## Favorite Coaching Moments

**Favorite memory from coaching:** Coaching JV at Lake Washington High School in 2001 — we didn't win a lot of games, but every single kid on the roster came back to play the next year. That's still the result I'm most proud of.

**One thing you've learned from coaching so far:** Kids will tell you what they need if you pay attention. Most coaching mistakes come from running the plan in your head instead of reading what's actually in front of you.
